\chapter{Electrical System Architecture and Embedded Design}
\label{chap:electrical}

\section{Overview}
\label{sec:electrical_overview}

This chapter details the design, implementation, and integration of the electrical subsystems for the AUSRA mobile robot platform. The electrical architecture is designed to support a robust, autonomous swarm capability by prioritizing three key engineering principles:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical Control:} Decoupling high-level AI processing from low-level real-time actuation ensures that computationally intensive tasks such as SLAM and path planning do not interfere with time-critical motor control loops.
    \item \textbf{Power Isolation:} Separating noisy motor loads from sensitive logic circuits prevents electromagnetic interference and voltage fluctuations from corrupting sensor data or causing computational errors.
    \item \textbf{Modular Safety:} Implementing physical hardware failsafes for charging and operation protects both the user and the electronic components from hazardous conditions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Topology}
\label{sec:system_topology}

The system is built upon a distributed power and control architecture. A central 20V DC bus, derived from a high-discharge Lithium-Ion source, powers two distinct regulation stages: the \textbf{Logic Stage} for computation and sensors, and the \textbf{Actuation Stage} for motor propulsion.

The control hierarchy consists of an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano serving as the high-level ``Brain'' (handling SLAM, path planning, and vision), which communicates via serial interface with an ESP32 Microcontroller, acting as the low-level ``Spine'' (handling inverse kinematics, PID control, and IMU data acquisition).

\subsubsection{Final Selection: Cytron MDD3A}

The \textbf{Cytron MDD3A} (Dual Channel 3A Motor Driver) was selected. The L298N was rejected for its BJT-based $\approx$2~V voltage drop (wasting $\sim$17\% of battery power as heat) and its 2.0~A continuous rating, which provides insufficient headroom for the motors' 1.5--1.8~A draw. \end{equation}

\textbf{Average Power:}

P_{avg} = 15 + 10.8 + 3 + 0.5 + 1 = 30.3 \approx 30 \text{ W}

\textbf{Maximum Power:}
\begin{equation}
P_{max} = 25 + 72 + 5 + 1 + 1 = 104 \approx 105 \text{ W}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Current Calculation}
Since the system is powered by a 20V source through Buck Converters, we calculate the current drawn from the battery, accounting for an estimated 90\% converter efficiency ($\eta = 0.9$).

\textbf{Average Current Draw:}
\begin{equation}
P_{batt,avg} = \frac{P_{avg}}{\eta} = \frac{30 \text{ W}}{0.9} = 33.3 \text{ W}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
I_{avg} = \frac{P_{batt,avg}}{V} = \frac{33.3 \text{ W}}{20 \text{ V}} = 1.67 \text{ A}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Peak Current Draw:}
\begin{equation}
P_{batt,max} = \frac{P_{max}}{\eta} = \frac{105 \text{ W}}{0.9} = 116.7 \text{ W}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
I_{max} = \frac{P_{batt,max}}{V} = \frac{116.7 \text{ W}}{20 \text{ V}} = 5.84 \text{ A}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Selected Battery Specifications:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model:} Total P20S (Lithium-Ion Power Tool Pack)
    \item \textbf{Voltage:} 20 V
    \item \textbf{Capacity:} 2000 mAh (2.0 Ah)
    \item \textbf{Discharge Rating:} High-Drain ($>$10C)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Runtime Calculation:}
\begin{equation}
\text{Runtime} = \frac{\text{Capacity}}{I_{avg}} = \frac{2.0 \text{ Ah}}{1.67 \text{ A}} = 1.2 \text{ h} \approx 72 \text{ minutes}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Result:} The 2000mAh pack provides approximately 72 minutes of continuous operation, exceeding the 30-minute requirement by a factor of 2.4.

\paragraph{C-Rating (Discharge Safety) Check}
We must ensure the battery can handle the peak current (during acceleration/stall conditions) without damage.

\textbf{Required C-Rating:}
\begin{equation}
C_{req} = \frac{I_{max}}{\text{Capacity}} = \frac{5.84 \text{ A}}{2.0 \text{ Ah}} = 2.92 \text{ C}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Conclusion:} The required 2.92C is extremely low compared to the battery's rated capability ($>$10C), ensuring the pack will run cool and safe even during aggressive maneuvering.

\section{Power Distribution and Safety Strategy}
\label{sec:power_distribution}

Effective power management is achieved through a combination of physical signal isolation and multi-stage voltage regulation.

\subsection{Physical Isolation Logic (Three-State Failsafe)}
\label{subsec:isolation_logic}

Safety is enforced via a \textbf{6-pin E-TEN1322 Toggle Switch (ON-OFF-ON)}. This component acts as a hardware multiplexer, defining three mutually exclusive operational states to prevent user error and electrical hazards.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\linewidth]{figures/Chapter 4/Power_switch.png}
    \caption{Three-State Power Switch Configuration}
    \label{fig:power_switch}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State 1: Charge Mode} --- The battery terminals are electrically disconnected from the robot's internal power bus and routed exclusively to the external GX16 Aviation Port. This ensures 100\% isolation of the sensitive computing units (Jetson/ESP32) from potential voltage spikes or ground loops during the charging cycle.
    
    \item \textbf{State 2: Idle} --- In the neutral position, the battery is fully disconnected from both the charging port and the internal system. This serves as a \textbf{Master Kill Switch}, preventing accidental startup during transport, maintenance, or code flashing. It also mitigates parasitic battery drain when the robot is in storage.
    
    \item \textbf{State 3: Run Mode} --- The charging port is physically disconnected, and the battery power is routed to the internal 20V DC Bus. This activates the dual-stage regulation system to power the robot's logic and actuation subsystems.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dual-Stage Voltage Regulation}
\label{subsec:voltage_regulation}

To maintain system stability and component safety, the 20V bus is conditioned by two independent regulation stages:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/Chapter 4/Buck_convertor.png}
    \caption{Buck Converter Modules for Voltage Regulation}
    \label{fig:buck_converter}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Logic Regulator:} A precision step-down converter provides a stable supply for the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. This isolation protects the compute stack from voltage ``brownouts'' that occur on the main line when motors draw high inrush currents during acceleration.
    
    \item \textbf{Actuation Regulator:} A dedicated high-power buck converter steps the 20V battery voltage down to a regulated 12V supply, matching the nominal voltage rating of the DC motors. This regulation guarantees that the motors receive a consistent voltage regardless of the battery's fluctuating charge level (21V to 18V), ensuring predictable kinematic performance throughout the entire discharge cycle.
\end{itemize}

\section{Low-Level Firmware Design}
\label{sec:lowlevel_design}

This section presents the theoretical foundation and implementation strategy for the low-level motor control firmware running on the ESP32-S3 microcontroller.

\subsection{Actuator Feedback: Quadrature Encoders}
\label{subsec:encoders}

Encoders are sensors mounted on a motor's rear shaft to sense rotation. As the shaft moves, the encoder generates a pulse train signal that provides position and velocity feedback for closed-loop control.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/Chapter 4/Motor_encoder_topology.jpg}
    \caption{Motor Encoder Topology}
    \label{fig:encoder_topology}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Operating Principle}

The JGY-370 motors include integrated optical quadrature encoders. After quadrature decoding under the X2 scheme, the AUSRA drive motors yield a total of 1997 counts per revolution (CPR); that is, 1997 counts accumulate over one complete revolution of the output shaft. The pulse frequency indicates rotational speed while the phase relationship between channels A and B determines direction.

\subsection{Encoder Data Acquisition Strategy}
\label{subsec:encoder_acquisition}

At the firmware's 1997 CPR and target speeds up to 300 RPM, the interrupt rate reaches approximately $1997 \times (300/60) \approx 9{,}985$ counts per second per motor---a new edge roughly every 100 microseconds. Across three motors this approaches 30,000 interrupts per second, so the acquisition strategy must impose minimal per-event overhead. Two architectural approaches were evaluated:

\subsubsection{Polling vs. Interrupt-Driven Architecture}

\paragraph{Polling (Rejected)}
In polling, the CPU continuously checks GPIO pin states in the main loop. However, the ESP32 must concurrently handle micro-ROS communication, telemetry publishing, and PID calculations. If any task blocks the CPU for longer than the inter-edge interval ($\approx$100$\mu$s at top speed), counts are missed, causing incorrect velocity readings and erratic behavior.

\paragraph{Interrupt Service Routines (Selected)}
We adopted an event-driven architecture using hardware interrupts. When a pin voltage transitions, the GPIO controller triggers the CPU to immediately execute an Interrupt Service Routine (ISR), increment the counter, and return to the main task. This guarantees atomic data capture with nanosecond-scale latency regardless of CPU load.

\paragraph{Critical Implementation: IRAM\_ATTR}
ESP32 program code typically resides in external Flash memory. If an interrupt fires while Flash is busy, the system crashes. The solution is the \texttt{IRAM\_ATTR} macro:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=C, caption={Direction-aware encoder ISR with IRAM\_ATTR for Flash-Independent Execution}]
void IRAM_ATTR isr1() {
    // Fires on every edge of channel A (CHANGE trigger)
    // Phase of channel B at that instant encodes direction
    if (digitalRead(M1_ENC_A) == digitalRead(M1_ENC_B)) { ticks1 = ticks1 + 1; }
    else { ticks1 = ticks1 - 1; }
}
\end{lstlisting}

This directive places the ISR code in internal SRAM, ensuring zero-latency access even during Flash operations.

\subsection{Quadrature Encoding Selection}
\label{subsec:quadrature_selection}

Quadrature encoders produce two channels (A and B) offset by 90°, enabling direction sensing. Three encoding types exist: X1, X2, and X4, differing in which edges are counted.

\subsubsection{X2 Encoding Selection Rationale}

The firmware adopts \textbf{X2 decoding}: a single interrupt is attached to Channel A on the \texttt{CHANGE} trigger, so the ISR fires on both the rising and falling edges of A, and the instantaneous level of Channel B is sampled to infer direction. This sits between the minimal X1 scheme (one edge of one channel) and full X4 (all four edges of both channels), and was chosen for three reasons:

\paragraph{1. Direction-Aware Counting at Half the X4 Load}
Because the ISR reads Channel B at the instant A changes, the sign of each increment is recovered directly from the phase relationship: when A and B read equal the shaft turns one way, and when they differ it turns the other (see the \texttt{isr1()} listing above). This yields signed counts without a second interrupt line per motor, halving the interrupt burden relative to X4 while retaining full direction sensing.

\paragraph{2. Computational Efficiency}
At 300 RPM with three motors, using the calibrated 1997 counts per revolution as the X2 post-decode resolution:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{X4 Mode:} approximately $3994 \times (300/60) \times 3 \approx 59{,}910$ ISR calls/second
    \item \textbf{X2 Mode:} approximately $1997 \times (300/60) \times 3 \approx 29{,}955$ ISR calls/second
\end{itemize}

X2 roughly halves the interrupt overhead of X4, preserving CPU cycles for the micro-ROS communication stack.

\paragraph{3. Sufficient Resolution}
At 1997 counts/rev ($\approx 0.18°$/count), the encoder provides ample granularity for smooth velocity estimation and PID control even at low shaft speeds, well above the resolution required by the control loop.

\subsection{Encoder Calibration}
\label{subsec:encoder_calibration}

Accurate counts-per-revolution (CPR) determination is critical for odometry, since this constant maps raw tick deltas to angular displacement and velocity. Calibration tracked rotation directly at the output shaft so that the figure includes the full gearbox reduction actually seen by the wheel.

\paragraph{Procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Mark a reference index on the output shaft and chassis
    \item Rotate the shaft exactly one revolution (360°) while recording the signed tick count
    \item Repeat 20 times per motor and average the results to reject manual-alignment error
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Result:} The calibration converged to \textbf{1997 counts per revolution (CPR)} under X2 decoding, which was hardcoded into the firmware as \texttt{TOTAL\_CPR = 1997}.

\subsection{Velocity Estimation and Signal Processing}
\label{subsec:velocity_estimation}

Deriving velocity $\omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt}$ from discrete encoder ticks introduces quantization noise, particularly problematic for the PID derivative term.

\subsubsection{The Quantization Problem}

With the control loop running every 30 ms (approximately 33 Hz), the raw tick count per cycle is an integer, so at steady speed it fluctuates by a single count between adjacent cycles. This one-count ``staircase'' quantization creates high-frequency spikes in the derivative calculation, causing motor humming and instability.

\subsubsection{Solution: First-Order Low-Pass Filter}

We implemented an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) filter:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\omega}[k] = \alpha \cdot \omega_{raw}[k] + (1-\alpha) \cdot \hat{\omega}[k-1]
\label{eq:lowpass_filter}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\hat{\omega}[k]$ = filtered velocity estimate
    \item $\omega_{raw}[k]$ = raw velocity from encoder ticks
    \item $\alpha$ = smoothing factor (0 to 1)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Tuning:} Through step response analysis, the smoothing factor was set to $\alpha = 0.45$ (the deployed firmware value, \texttt{LPF\_ALPHA = 0.45}). This weighting favours the freshest measurement enough to keep the velocity estimate responsive, while still averaging out the single-count quantization jitter that would otherwise corrupt the derivative term. The resulting phase lag is small relative to the mechanical time constant of the loaded wheel, so closed-loop stability margins are preserved.

\section{Control Strategy and Controller Selection}
\label{sec:control_strategy}

To regulate the angular velocity of the omnidirectional wheels, a comparative analysis of three standard control architectures was conducted: Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID), Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR), and Model Predictive Control (MPC). The selection criteria focused on computational efficiency, model dependency, and suitability for the ESP32-S3 hardware constraints.

\subsubsection{Selection Rationale}
MPC was rejected because solving a Quadratic Programming problem at every time step is computationally infeasible on an ESP32-S3 running three parallel control loops. LQR requires an accurate state-space plant model (inductance, moment of inertia, friction); these parameters are unknown for the loaded vehicle and change under varying floor conditions. PID was selected as the industry-standard SISO setpoint-tracking controller: it requires only basic arithmetic, is model-free, and is well-understood in the embedded motor-control community.

\subsection{Mathematical Derivation: Discrete PID Algorithm}
\label{subsec:discrete_pid}

The standard continuous-time PID equation is:
\begin{equation}
    u(t) = K_p \cdot e(t) + K_i \int e(t) \, dt + K_d \cdot \frac{de(t)}{dt}
    \label{eq:pid_continuous}
\end{equation}
Discretized using the Backward Euler method with sampling period $T_s = 0.03$~s ($\approx$33~Hz), the control law implemented per wheel is:
\begin{equation}
    u[k] = K_p\,e[k] + K_i \cdot \mathrm{clamp}\!\left(I_{acc}[k-1] + e[k]\cdot T_s,\ -75,\ +75\right) + K_d \cdot \frac{e[k]-e[k-1]}{T_s}
    \label{eq:pid_discrete_total}
\end{equation}
where $e[k]$ is the RPM error at tick $k$. The integral accumulator is clamped to $[-75, +75]$ before the gain is applied, constituting the anti-windup mechanism (Section~\ref{subsec:anti_windup}).

\subsection{Stability Augmentation: Anti-Windup Strategy}
\label{subsec:anti_windup}

A critical limitation of the standard Integral term is \textit{windup}. When the actuator reaches its physical limit (e.g., Max PWM = 255) but the error persists (e.g., the robot is blocked by an obstacle), the Integral term continues to accumulate virtually to infinity. When the obstacle is removed, the controller remains saturated for a prolonged period, causing dangerous overshooting.

To mitigate this, a \textbf{clamping anti-windup} mechanism is applied directly to the integral accumulator. Rather than clamping the final PWM output and conditionally freezing the integrator, the firmware bounds the accumulated integral state itself on every iteration, before the integral gain is applied:

\begin{equation}
    I_{acc}[k] = \mathrm{clamp}\left( I_{acc}[k-1] + e[k] \cdot T_s,\ -75,\ +75 \right)
    \label{eq:anti_windup}
\end{equation}

The accumulator $I_{acc}$ can therefore never grow without bound while the actuator is saturated against a blocked wheel; it is hard-limited to the interval $[-75, +75]$. The instant the obstruction clears, the integral term is already within range, so the controller recovers immediately instead of unwinding a large stored error. This corresponds exactly to the \texttt{constrain(\_integral + error * dt, -75, 75)} statement in \texttt{PIDController::compute()} (Appendix A).

\subsection{Dead-Zone Compensation}
\label{subsec:deadzone}

A brushed DC gear-motor does not respond to arbitrarily small PWM duty cycles: below a threshold the magnetic torque cannot overcome static friction and the rotor stalls silently. Driving the motor directly with the raw PID output therefore wastes the lower portion of the command range and produces a dead band around zero velocity. The firmware compensates for this in \texttt{Motor::drive()} by remapping any non-trivial command onto the actuated portion of the duty-cycle range:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PWM} = \mathrm{map}(|u|,\ 0,\ 255,\ \mathrm{minPWM},\ 255)
    \label{eq:deadzone_map}
\end{equation}

where $u$ is the PID output and $\mathrm{minPWM} = 30$ is the per-motor breakaway duty cycle. As soon as the controller requests motion, the smallest emitted command is the value that actually starts the wheel turning, which linearises the effective actuator response and sharpens low-speed tracking. When the target velocity is zero the motor is hard-stopped (both half-bridge inputs driven low) so the robot holds position cleanly rather than creeping on residual integral error.

\subsection{Tuning Methodology}
\label{subsec:pid_tuning}

Because the low-level firmware was developed in parallel with the mechanical assembly, the plant mass and inertia were not fixed during early bring-up, which ruled out a single model-based design. A pragmatic two-stage empirical procedure was adopted instead, moving from an isolated, repeatable condition to the fully loaded robot. The \texttt{setGains()} method exposed by \texttt{PIDController} (Appendix A) was the enabling tool: it allows the gains to be overwritten live over the micro-ROS link and resets the integral accumulator on each update, so a candidate gain set can be evaluated immediately without reflashing the ESP32-S3.

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Free-Air Tuning (Unloaded)}
The first stage was performed by hanging the robot so that all three wheels rotated freely without load. Isolating the motors removes the $5$\,kg vehicle inertia and floor friction, leaving only the motor-and-gearbox dynamics to establish baseline $K_p$, $K_i$, and $K_d$ values for stable velocity. Starting from $K_i = K_d = 0$, $K_p$ was raised until measured RPM tracked a step setpoint with a fast rise. $K_i$ was then introduced to eliminate steady-state error, and a small $K_d$ was added to damp residual overshoot.

\subsubsection{Stage 2: On-Ground Tuning (Loaded)}
The provisional free-air gains were then re-evaluated with the fully assembled robot on the floor. This fine-tuning stage is critical to compensate for the chassis weight, full vehicle inertia, and real-world floor friction. Using the \texttt{setGains()} live-update interface, the gains were trimmed on-the-fly during translation and rotation commands until wheel velocities settled quickly without sustained oscillation. The final committed values were:

\begin{equation}
    K_p = 10, \qquad K_i = 10, \qquad K_d = 0.01
    \label{eq:final_gains}
\end{equation}

with the integral accumulator clamped to $[-75, +75]$ (Section~\ref{subsec:anti_windup}) and a per-motor minimum drive of $\mathrm{minPWM} = 30$. The comparatively low derivative gain reflects the noise sensitivity of the differentiated encoder signal, while the matched proportional and integral gains give a responsive loop that still eliminates steady-state error against rolling resistance.

\section{The Micro-ROS Architecture}
\label{sec:microros}

This section explains the communication bridge between the MCU and the high-level computer (Jetson/Laptop).

\subsection{Overview: Bridging Real-Time Embedded Systems with DDS}
\label{subsec:microros_overview}

The integration of resource-constrained microcontrollers (MCUs) into the Robot Operating System (ROS 2) ecosystem presents a fundamental architectural challenge. Standard ROS 2 nodes communicate using the Data Distribution Service (DDS) protocol---specifically RTPS (Real-Time Publish-Subscribe). DDS is a heavy, peer-to-peer protocol requiring significant memory for discovery caches, serialization buffers, and history scaling, often exceeding 1MB of RAM---far beyond the capacity of typical MCUs.

To resolve this, \textbf{micro-ROS} was implemented, a specialized variant designed to extend the DDS ``Global Data Space'' onto the ESP32-S3. Unlike simple serial bridges (e.g., rosserial) which use custom, non-standard framing, micro-ROS creates first-class ROS 2 nodes on the microcontroller, allowing them to interact seamlessly with the high-level system using standard message definitions.

\subsection{The Client-Agent Architecture}
\label{subsec:client_agent}

The micro-ROS Client running on the ESP32-S3 serializes messages using Micro-XRCE-DDS and forwards them point-to-point to the Micro-ROS Agent process on the Jetson. The Agent translates these into standard DDS-RTPS packets and registers the MCU's publishers and subscribers in the ROS~2 graph, acting as a stateful proxy. If the Agent crashes, all MCU-managed entities immediately disappear from the ROS graph, providing an inherent liveness check.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/Chapter 4/Micro_ros_architecture.png}
    \caption{Micro-ROS Client-Agent Architecture}
    \label{fig:microros_architecture}
\end{figure}

micro-ROS uses the Micro-XRCE-DDS protocol for lightweight, CDR-serialized message transport with a $<$50~KB RAM footprint, and supports both wired (USB-CDC serial at 115200~baud, the deployed configuration) and wireless (UDP/Wi-Fi, development only) transports through a single \texttt{set\_microros\_transports()} call.

\subsection{Agent Deployment}
\label{subsec:agent_deployment}

In the deployed configuration, a helper script (\texttt{start\_micro\_ros\_agent.sh}) resets the ESP32-S3 via \texttt{esptool}, transmits the robot namespace, then launches the Agent against the USB-CDC device:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={Micro-ROS Agent Launch (Deployment)}]
ros2 run micro_ros_agent micro_ros_agent serial \
     --dev /dev/ttyACM0 -b 115200
\end{lstlisting}

Publishers and subscribers use \texttt{rmw\_qos\_profile\_sensor\_data} (Best Effort): packets are sent once and discarded if lost, rather than queued for retransmission --- critical for real-time velocity commands where a late command is worse than no command.

\subsection{Section Summary}
\label{subsec:microros_summary}

This architecture effectively creates a distributed computing system where the ``Brain'' (Jetson) and ``Muscle'' (ESP32) are loosely coupled by a standardized protocol (DDS-XRCE), yet tightly integrated in terms of data flow. The use of the Agent as an intermediary allows the heavy ROS 2 stack to remain on the host, while the Client remains lightweight enough to run alongside the time-critical control loop. On the ESP32-S3 these concerns are split across the two cores: telemetry is published at 50~Hz while the PID control loop runs independently at $\sim$33~Hz (30~ms period), as detailed in Section~\ref{sec:firmware_architecture}.

\section{Firmware Architecture and Real-Time Implementation}
\label{sec:firmware_architecture}

The deployed firmware (single source of truth: the \texttt{low-level/FREERTOS/} package) is a dual-core FreeRTOS application that cleanly separates soft real-time communication from hard real-time motor control. This section documents the final architecture and the reasoning that produced it.

\subsection{Architectural Iteration}
\label{subsec:arch_iteration}

The low-level subsystem converged through three architectural explorations before reaching its final form. An initial RTOS-native attempt---embedding micro-ROS as a prioritized task directly within an ESP-IDF FreeRTOS build---was abandoned because the XRCE-DDS serialization layer proved stack-intensive and the bespoke kernel build was difficult to maintain. A second, bare-metal ESP-IDF approach using the hardware pulse-counter (PCNT) and LED-control (LEDC) peripherals minimized CPU load but required reimplementing PID, kinematics, and vector math from scratch, with brittle register-level configuration. The final hybrid architecture retains the Arduino core for rapid hardware abstraction while running micro-ROS for communication, splitting the workload across the ESP32-S3's two cores. This combination delivered the development velocity of the Arduino ecosystem with the determinism of explicit core affinity, and is the architecture described in the remainder of this section.

\subsection{Dual-Core Task Model (FreeRTOS Integration)}
\label{subsec:dualcore_tasks}

FreeRTOS is a real-time operating system that provides deterministic task scheduling and resource management. By implementing FreeRTOS on the ESP32-S3, the firmware partitions its workload across the two Xtensa LX7 cores using asymmetric priorities and fixed core affinity. This marks a significant architectural enhancement over a standard Arduino ``superloop'', where communication delays would inevitably block motor control.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{microROSTask}} (Core 0, priority 1): runs the micro-ROS communication stack, servicing subscriptions and publishing telemetry at a fixed 50~Hz cadence.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{pidControlTask}} (Core 1, priority 5): executes the strict real-time PID control loop every 30~ms ($\sim$33~Hz). Separating the control loop onto a dedicated core guarantees system determinism and absolute loop-rate stability, completely insulated from network jitter.
\end{itemize}

The two tasks exchange data through a single mutex-guarded \texttt{SharedRobotData} structure: command targets flow downward (executor $\rightarrow$ control loop) and joint position/velocity telemetry flows upward (control loop $\rightarrow$ executor). The mutex bounds the critical section to the brief struct copy, so neither task blocks the other for a meaningful fraction of its period.

\subsection{Control Task Walkthrough}
\label{subsec:pid_task_walkthrough}

On each 30~ms tick, \texttt{pidControlTask} performs the following sequence:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Acquire targets:} take the mutex and copy the latest commanded wheel velocities out of \texttt{SharedRobotData}.
    \item \textbf{Ramp limiting:} apply the slew-rate limiter (Section~\ref{subsec:ramp_limiter}) to bound the change in setpoint per tick.
    \item \textbf{Feedback:} read each encoder count inside a \texttt{noInterrupts()}/\texttt{interrupts()} critical section, convert to RPM, and smooth with the exponential moving-average filter ($\alpha = 0.45$).
    \item \textbf{PID update:} compute the per-wheel control effort with the discrete PID law and integral anti-windup clamp.
    \item \textbf{Actuate:} map the control effort through the minimum-PWM dead-zone compensation and drive the Cytron MDD3A channels.
    \item \textbf{Publish telemetry:} write the measured joint positions and velocities back into \texttt{SharedRobotData} for the executor to publish.
\end{enumerate}

Encoder ISRs are tagged \texttt{IRAM\_ATTR} so they reside in internal SRAM, eliminating flash cache-miss stalls and keeping interrupt latency minimal. Shared encoder counters are read atomically:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=C, caption={Atomic encoder read}]
noInterrupts();  // Critical section start
long safe_counts = encoder_count;
interrupts();    // Critical section end
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Slew-Rate (Ramp) Limiting}
\label{subsec:ramp_limiter}

During initial testing, applying a raw step input (an instant target velocity) caused the robot to slightly rotate off-axis at start-up. This occurred because the three wheels, facing different physical resistance, accelerated at slightly different rates. To resolve this, the firmware implements a slew-rate limiter (ramp input) to bound the maximum permissible change per tick:

\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\max} = a_{\max} \, \Delta t,
\label{eq:ramp_max_change}
\end{equation}

where $a_{\max} = 50~\mathrm{RPM/s}$ and $\Delta t = 30~\mathrm{ms}$. Rather than clamping each wheel independently---which would distort the commanded motion direction---the firmware computes the largest required change across all three wheels, $\Delta_{\max,\text{req}}$, and derives a single scale factor:

\begin{equation}
s = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{\Delta_{\max}}{\Delta_{\max,\text{req}}}\right),
\label{eq:ramp_scale}
\end{equation}

which is then applied uniformly to all three wheel setpoint increments. This proportional scaling is critical: it maintains the exact mathematical difference between wheel velocities at all times, preserving the intended directional vector from a dead stop.

\subsection{PID Implementation and Tuning}
\label{subsec:pid_impl}

Each wheel runs the discrete PID law of Section~\ref{subsec:discrete_pid} with the integral anti-windup clamp of Section~\ref{subsec:anti_windup} and the dead-zone compensation of Section~\ref{subsec:deadzone}. The deployed gains, obtained through the two-stage methodology of Section~\ref{subsec:pid_tuning}, are $K_p = 10$, $K_i = 10$, and $K_d = 0.01$. A \texttt{setGains()} entry point allows these constants to be updated at run time, which was used during on-ground loaded tuning.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:electrical_summary}

This chapter has presented the complete electrical architecture and embedded software design of the AUSRA robot platform. The key contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{System Architecture:} A hierarchical control topology was established with the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano as the high-level ``Brain'' and the ESP32-S3 as the low-level ``Spine,'' enabling decoupled AI processing from real-time motor control.
    
    \item \textbf{Component Selection:} Through weighted decision matrices:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The \textbf{ESP32-S3} was selected for its micro-ROS compatibility, dual-core architecture, and cost efficiency (Score: 8.80/10)
        \item The \textbf{Cytron MDD3A} provides MOSFET efficiency with 50\% current headroom and integrated protection (Score: 63)
        \item The \textbf{Total P20S 20V battery} offers integrated BMS, charge-in-place capability, and 72-minute runtime (Score: 9.6/10)
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Power Management:} A three-state hardware switch (Charge/Idle/Run) ensures operational safety, while dual-stage buck conversion isolates logic from motor noise.
    
    \item \textbf{Low-Level Firmware:} Quadrature encoders (1997 CPR) with X2 decoding provide velocity feedback through IRAM-resident ISRs, processed by an EMA filter ($\alpha = 0.45$) for noise rejection.
    
    \item \textbf{Control Strategy:} A discrete PID controller was selected over LQR and MPC for its model-free operation and computational efficiency, with clamping anti-windup for stability. Tuning employed a two-stage empirical methodology: free-air unloaded step response to set initial gains, followed by on-ground loaded refinement via the \texttt{setGains()} live-update interface, yielding $K_p=10$, $K_i=10$, $K_d=0.01$.
    
    \item \textbf{Micro-ROS Integration:} The Client-Agent architecture bridges the ESP32 to the ROS~2 ecosystem via Micro-XRCE-DDS, with Best Effort QoS ensuring smooth real-time velocity command delivery over the USB-serial micro-ROS link.
    
    \item \textbf{Real-Time Architecture:} The final dual-core FreeRTOS implementation cleanly decouples the 50~Hz micro-ROS telemetry task (Core~0) from the $\sim$33~Hz PID control loop (Core~1), ensuring neither task starves the other during sustained autonomous operation.
\end{enumerate}

These design choices collectively enable the AUSRA platform to achieve reliable autonomous operation while maintaining the modularity, safety, and real-time performance required for swarm robotics research.
