\section{Introduction to the Hardware Architecture}
\label{sec:hardware_intro}

The hardware architecture of the Ausra swarm is designed to bridge the gap between physical robustness and high-level autonomous intelligence \cite{siegwart2011introduction}. To achieve collaborative 2D mapping, the mechanical structure must serve as a stable, mobile platform for sensitive electronic sensors while maintaining the agility required for swarm formations.

\subsection{Design Requirements and Constraints}
In the context of collaborative 2D mapping, the mechanical structure must serve as a stable, mobile platform for sensitive electronic sensors. The following requirements were established to ensure the swarm can navigate indoor environments efficiently.

The 5~kg weight ceiling keeps motor torque requirements low and extends battery endurance on longer mapping missions. A minimum linear speed of 0.2~m/s was chosen to balance floor-plan coverage rate against LiDAR sampling density — faster movement risks motion blur in the point cloud. The 30~cm diameter constraint ensures passage through standard doorways and is the key driver of the circular chassis geometry. A stackable three-layer architecture satisfies the modularity requirement, allowing individual components (battery, ESP32, sensors) to be swapped without disturbing adjacent layers.

\subsection{Functional Layer Decomposition}
The hardware is organised into three stacked layers: a \textbf{Locomotion \& Actuation} base (chassis, motors, motor drivers), a \textbf{Low-Level Control} middle tier (ESP32, encoders, power management), and a \textbf{Perception \& High-Level Computing} top tier (Jetson Orin Nano, LiDAR, OAK-D camera). Physical separation of high-current actuation from logic circuits is the primary EMI-mitigation strategy throughout the design.

\section{Robot's Hardware Selection}
\label{sec:hardware_selection}

After defining the main requirements and the hardware architecture, this section would demonstrate how we selected all hardware components for our robot starting from the selection of wheels for maneuverability of the swarm robots moving together to the high-level board for the autonomous tasks execution.

\subsection{Locomotion \& Actuation Group}
The locomotion and actuation group constitutes the physical "base" layer of the Ausra robot. This group is responsible for translating digital control signals into precise mechanical movement \cite{corke2017robotics}. To meet the requirements of holonomic motion and a 5kg weight limit, the following components were selected through a series of trade-off analyses.

\subsubsection{Locomotion System (Wheel Selection)}
An omnidirectional locomotion system was selected for the platform. The 3-wheel 120\degree\ configuration provides full 3-DOF holonomic motion, essential for the swarm's collaborative tasks such as map merging and tight-formation maneuvering. This setup minimizes the actuator count compared to a 4-wheel mecanum drive, lowering per-unit cost and weight. Unlike mecanum wheels, which rely on 45\degree\ rollers that often induce longitudinal vibrations and slippage on smooth floors, standard omni-wheels feature 90\degree\ peripheral rollers. These orthogonal rollers allow for smooth, vibration-free lateral sliding on flat indoor surfaces, significantly reducing the noise injected into the inertial and odometric sensors.


\subsubsection{Chassis Geometry \& Configuration}
The chassis serves as the structural backbone of the robot, providing the mounting surfaces for the actuation group, the electronic stack, and the perception sensors. A circular geometry with a 30~cm diameter was selected over rectangular or triangular alternatives. The primary engineering justification for this shape is its rotational invariance: a circular chassis possesses a constant sweep radius. This means the robot's footprint in the 2D occupancy map remains strictly invariant under rotation, a critical property that vastly simplifies the obstacle avoidance and path planning algorithms. When the robot must spin freely in confined spaces or tight corridors, the navigation stack does not need to compute dynamic boundary collisions. Furthermore, the 30~cm circular plate maximizes the usable surface area relative to its maximum width, allowing the heavy battery and electronic stack to be positioned tightly around the central axis, minimizing the moment of inertia and keeping the center of gravity (CoG) perfectly balanced.


\newpage

\subsubsection{Material Selection of Robot Plate}

Acrylic was selected as the primary plate material due to its low density, electrical insulation properties, and high manufacturability. While metals like aluminium offer higher stiffness-to-weight ratios, they act as conductive planes that can short exposed PCB traces and interfere with the Wi-Fi/DDS signals crucial for swarm communication. Acrylic provides a lightweight, rigid structural base that is highly transparent to radio frequencies and can be rapidly prototyped using laser cutting for sub-millimeter precision. 

In addition to the flat acrylic plates, the robot's cylindrical outer wall and internal fixations are 3D-printed in Polylactic Acid (PLA). Additive manufacturing enables the complex integrated geometry necessary to position the three motor mounts at precise 120\degree\ intervals, something that would be prohibitively expensive to machine out of metal. The combination of laser-cut acrylic planes and 3D-printed PLA joints creates a modular, non-conductive, and highly repeatable physical architecture across all swarm agents.

\subsubsection{Actuator Sizing and Motor Selection}
The selection of actuators is a critical phase in the mechanical design of the Ausrabot robot. The motors must provide sufficient torque to overcome static friction and inertia while maintaining a rotational speed that meets the minimum linear velocity requirement of 0.2 m/s. This section outlines the requirements determined by the robot's kinematics and justifies the final component choice through a weighted selection matrix.

\paragraph{A. Determination of Motor Requirements}
To size the actuators correctly, the desired robot performance must be mapped to the individual wheel requirements. Unlike a standard differential drive, a 3-wheel omni-directional configuration dictates that wheel speeds are vector components of the robot's total velocity. The full kinematic model that formalises this mapping (the inverse-kinematics transformation matrix) is derived in Section~\ref{subsec:omni_driver}; here it is used only to extract the worst-case speed and torque the motors must deliver.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Kinematic Velocity Requirements:} For a wheel of radius $r = 0.0325$~m, the angular speed required to produce a contact-point (rim) speed $v_w$ is $\omega_w = v_w / r$. Pure translation at the target $v = 0.2$~m/s requires, in the worst-case heading, a single wheel to carry the full body speed, so
    \begin{equation}
        n_{trans} = \frac{60\,v}{2\pi r} = \frac{60 \times 0.2}{2\pi \times 0.0325} \approx 59~\text{RPM}.
        \label{eq:rpm_translation}
    \end{equation}
    Simultaneous rotation adds a tangential term $R\,\omega_z$ at each wheel (base radius $R = 0.124$~m). Reserving a modest rotational margin of $\omega_z \approx 0.8$~rad/s adds $R\,\omega_z \approx 0.10$~m/s of rim speed, raising the requirement to
    \begin{equation}
        n_{req} = \frac{60\,(v + R\,\omega_z)}{2\pi r} \approx 85\text{--}90~\text{RPM}.
        \label{eq:rpm_total}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Calculated Minimum Motor RPM:} 85--90 RPM (combined translation and rotation, from Equation~\ref{eq:rpm_total}).
        \item \textbf{Target Linear Velocity ($v$):} $> 0.2$~m/s.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Torque Requirements:} The motor must overcome the inertial force ($F_i = m a$) and the rolling resistance ($F_{rr} = m g\,C_{rr}$). Given the Nylon rollers of the Omni-wheels on indoor flooring, a rolling resistance coefficient $C_{rr} = 0.02$ is assumed for a maximum robot mass $m = 5$~kg, with a startup acceleration $a = 0.2$~m/s$^2$:
    \begin{equation}
        F_{total} = m a + m g\,C_{rr} = 5(0.2) + 5(9.81)(0.02) \approx 1.98~\text{N}.
        \label{eq:total_force}
    \end{equation}
    Distributed across the three wheels and applied at the wheel radius, the per-wheel torque is
    \begin{equation}
        \tau_w = \frac{F_{total}\,r}{3} \approx \frac{1.98 \times 0.0325}{3} \approx 0.021~\text{N\,m} \approx 0.22~\text{kg\,cm}.
        \label{eq:wheel_torque}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Total Force ($F_{total}$):} Approximately 1.98~N (Equation~\ref{eq:total_force}).
        \item \textbf{Required Rated Torque:} The bare rolling demand is modest ($\approx 0.22$~kg\,cm per wheel); to absorb stall events, slopes, surface irregularities, and a generous safety factor, a rated torque of at least 0.8--1.0~kg\,cm per motor is specified.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

Based on these kinematic and torque requirements, the JGY-370 Worm Gear motor (98 RPM) was selected as the optimal actuator. The integrated worm-gear reduction provides the necessary high-rated torque to reliably propel the 5~kg platform under full load, absorbing physical shocks without stripping gears. The 98 RPM output speed provides sufficient headroom to achieve the 0.2~m/s target velocity while remaining in the motor's highest efficiency band. 

Beyond raw torque, the JGY-370 offers two critical architectural advantages. First, the worm-gear mechanism is inherently self-locking: it mechanically holds the robot's position without consuming electrical power for active braking. This static holding capability is highly beneficial for static 2D map-merging tasks and maintaining rigid swarm formations. Second, the right-angle form factor of the gearbox means the motor body sits parallel to the chassis edge, fitting perfectly within the 30~cm diameter constraint while leaving the central hub clear for the battery and electronics.

\section{Kinematic Hardware Summary}
\label{sec:cad_design}

The physical realization of the Ausra robot follows a modular stack architecture, separating high-current power, low-level logic, and high-level perception to minimize interference and optimize weight distribution.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Base Layer (Locomotion):} The primary acrylic base plate serves as the high-torque foundation, anchoring the three JGY-370 motors at precise 120\degree\ radial intervals. It acts as the high-current "Engine Room", localizing the Cytron motor drivers and a 12V buck converter near the actuators to minimize parasitic inductance and EMI crosstalk.
    \item \textbf{Mid Layer (Control Hub):} The intermediate layer houses the heavy 20V battery to maintain a low center of gravity. The ESP32-S3 microcontroller is mounted here, physically separated from the noisy motor wiring to maintain clean signal integrity for the micro-ROS serial line.
    \item \textbf{Top Layer (Perception):} The apex of the stack is dedicated entirely to spatial reasoning. The NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano, OAK-D camera, and 2D LiDAR are mounted centrally to provide an unobstructed 360\degree\ horizontal scan and maintain rotational invariance for the SLAM algorithms. The IMU is placed directly on the vertical axis of rotation to eliminate centripetal acceleration noise.
\end{itemize}

A 3D-printed PLA outer shell protects the sensitive internal electronics from external impacts and provides a uniform physical boundary for the swarm.



\section{Kinematic Modeling and URDF Generation}
\label{sec:urdf_generation}

The transition from a high-fidelity static SolidWorks assembly to a dynamic, simulation-ready robot is a defining phase in the development of the AUSRA swarm robots. In the context of modern mechatronics, the Unified Robot Description Format (URDF) serves as the ``single source of truth'' for the robot's physical properties, kinematic chains, and visual representation.

\subsection{Kinematic Tree Construction and Geometric Pre-processing}

The SolidWorks assembly was exported using the \texttt{sw2urdf} plugin. Because a 3-wheel omnidirectional drive requires precise 120\degree\ radial symmetry in its joint frames, a manual pre-processing step was essential: reference points were placed at the geometric centre of every motor shaft, wheel, and sensor, and each wheel's joint coordinate frame was aligned so that its Z-axis is perpendicular to the wheel face and its X-axis points along the wheel's forward-roll direction. The parent link is \texttt{base\_link} (chassis body and cover); each omni-wheel is a continuous-joint child, and the LiDAR and camera are fixed-joint children. Internal components not affecting simulation dynamics were omitted to keep the package lightweight.

\subsection{ROS 2 Package Migration}

The SolidWorks-exported package was migrated to ROS~2 Humble by rewriting the build system to use \texttt{ament\_cmake}, reconfiguring \texttt{package.xml} with modern dependencies (\texttt{robot\_state\_publisher}, \texttt{joint\_state\_publisher}, \texttt{xacro}), and developing Python launch files for RViz2 TF verification and Gazebo spawning. Inertial properties for each link were populated from SolidWorks' Mass Properties tool, and visual meshes were exported as \texttt{.stl} files for high-fidelity rendering at low computational overhead. The resulting \texttt{ausrabot\_description} package serves as the single source of truth for the robot's kinematic and visual description across simulation and hardware, and is the foundation on which the navigation stack of Chapter~\ref{chap:autonomous} is built.
